## CONTEXT
This is a live ladder stream where Wolfey is climbing from Master Ball 4 toward Champion tier in Pokemon Champions. He's using a Meganium team he built previously (from a YouTube video), making live modifications between games. The stream captures ~10+ games of real-time decision-making.

---

## PATTERN 1: Live Team Modification Between Games (Hot-Swapping)

Wolfey modifies his team BETWEEN games on the ladder, not in a vacuum:

- **Game 1 start:** He's debating whether to swap Archaludon for Aegislash. "Do I want to use the same Archaludon I've been using or do I want to change it up?" He starts with Aegislash.
- **After Game 1 loss:** "I think I need Archaludon back because I don't have a good lead right now, unless it's a mega Pokemon." Immediately swaps Aegislash → Archaludon.
- **Item deliberation for Archaludon:** Goes through available items by elimination: "White herb, no is already used. Lum berry isn't used. That seems really bad. Chople berry, dragon fang, these are all garbage... Citrus berry hasn't been used. Is it bad that I think bright powder is correct?" Settles on Leftovers.
- **Leftovers verdict evolves:** Initially dismissive ("I was kind of clowning on leftovers"), then surprised ("it's actually been really good here"), then convinced ("Archaludon healing so much over the course of the game made a really big difference"), then enthusiastic ("Stamina plus Leftovers is so good" — though he corrects himself that it's Sturdy, not Stamina).

**Key observation:** He changes ONE variable (Aegislash → Archaludon), tests it, evaluates. He doesn't rebuild the whole team after a loss.

## PATTERN 2: Team Preview Regret / Self-Correction

Wolfey frequently second-guesses his Team Preview decisions MID-GAME and learns from them:

- **Game 1 (vs Charizard):** "I actually think that I was supposed to bring Meganium here. I defaulted into Kangaskhan, but I actually think that I was supposed to bring Meganium... Meganium is good into five of these Pokemon and just because they have Charizard, I didn't bring it. That doesn't feel correct."
- **Lesson articulated:** Don't let a single bad-matchup Pokemon scare you off the correct bring when your star is good into 5/6 of their team.
- **Later (vs Floette team):** "I once again don't think I can bring Meganium into Sneezler." He correctly benches Meganium here.
- **vs Trick Room team:** "I have to lead Meganium because of the trick room threat." Overrides his instinct to lead Archaludon.
- **vs Sun team (later):** Brings Kangaskhan instead of Meganium without hesitation. "Against Sun, you can't bring Meganium. That's just the rule."
- **vs Sableye team:** "I brought Meganium because of the Sableye." Then later: "I should have brought Kangaskhan. Why didn't I bring Kangaskhan?"
- **Self-awareness about rushing:** "I need to take longer [in Team Preview]. I should have taken longer."

**Key observation:** He develops matchup-specific bring rules through live experience, not theory. The rules emerge from regret ("I was supposed to bring X") and get refined game by game.

## PATTERN 3: Scouting Through "Passive" Plays

Wolfey deliberately makes plays that look passive but are information-gathering:

- **Double Protect to scout:** "We're going to protect both of our Pokemon. This looks like a passive do nothing play, but in actuality, it does two things. The first is scout for what move Basculegion wants to lock into, and the second one is scout for Delphox Protect."
- **Confirming Choice Scarf:** "We confirm that it's scarf, by the way, because the Basculegion attacked before their Aerodactyl."
- **Reading opponent's last Pokemon:** Constantly tracking what he hasn't seen: "The thing is we haven't seen their last Pokemon yet."
- **Predicting based on team composition:** "I assume this is flip turn" — and it is. "I expect them to go for a rock slide plus flip turn here."

**Key observation:** Information gathering is treated as a real action, not wasted turns. He explicitly narrates the value of scouting plays.

## PATTERN 4: Item Scarcity as a Real Constraint (Champions-Specific)

The Champions format has a limited item pool, and Wolfey treats this as a genuine teambuilding constraint:

- "Why are there no items in this game?"
- Goes through items by elimination: White Herb taken, Lum Berry bad, Chople Berry/Dragon Fang "garbage"
- Considers Bright Powder (!) before settling on Leftovers
- Leftovers chosen for a specific mechanical reason: "It can help me get back to Sturdy" (Archaludon's ability)
- The item choice turns out to be game-winning: Leftovers + Sturdy creates a loop where Archaludon heals back above Sturdy threshold after taking hits

**Key observation:** Item selection by elimination under scarcity, not by ideal choice. The "bad" item (Leftovers) turns out to synergize with the ability (Sturdy) in a way he didn't fully anticipate.

## PATTERN 5: Mega Evolution Timing as a Habit/Mistake

Wolfey catches himself making a timing error with mega evolution:

- "I should have mega. I should have mega. That's a bad habit. It's cuz in with Tera you always delay but with mega you don't delay."
- This happens multiple times across games — he forgets to mega evolve on the first possible turn because his muscle memory is from Scarlet/Violet where you delay Terastallization.
- He explicitly identifies this as a format-transition habit that needs correction.

**Key observation:** Even world champions have mechanical habits from previous formats that create errors. He names the habit and its cause rather than just saying "oops."

## PATTERN 6: Emotional Regulation Under Pressure

Wolfey has a visible self-talk pattern for managing tilt:

- After bad RNG: "That's okay. It's okay. It's okay. It's good. That's okay. That's okay. That's okay. That's okay." (repeated mantra)
- After a misplay: "I was way too greedy. I should have protected." (immediate diagnosis, no dwelling)
- After a loss: "I think I need Archaludon" (immediately converts loss into actionable change)
- After a close win: "This Tauros popped off. This Tauros was MVP." (celebrates what worked)
- After getting distracted by a story and misplaying: "I got distracted by my story. I lost." (honest self-assessment)
- Recognizing when he's tired: "I'm just pretty tired to be honest... I was trying to build a mega Crawdaunt team but it didn't get to a point where I felt comfortable with it."

**Key observation:** The emotional pattern is: acknowledge → diagnose → act. Never: dwell → blame → tilt.

## PATTERN 7: Matchup-Specific Lead Sheets (Developed Live)

Wolfey develops lead preferences through trial and error during the stream:

- **vs Charizard teams:** Kangaskhan + Aerodactyl (Fake Out + Tailwind), but realizes Meganium might be better if Charizard is only 1/6 threats
- **vs Floette teams:** Aerodactyl + Archaludon (Flash Cannon / Steel Beam to hit Floette through redirection)
- **vs Trick Room:** Must lead Meganium (to threaten TR setters with its speed + coverage)
- **vs Rain:** Meganium lead (Solar Beam + Weather Ball are strong)
- **vs Sun:** Kangaskhan lead (Meganium can't be brought at all)
- **vs Sableye:** Meganium lead (to threaten Sableye specifically)
- **Default non-mega lead:** Aerodactyl + Archaludon

**Key observation:** He doesn't have a pre-made lead sheet. He builds one in real-time through experience, and it evolves across the stream.

## PATTERN 8: Recognizing When a Pokemon "Can't Be Brought"

A recurring decision pattern: explicitly ruling out the star Pokemon for certain matchups:

- "Against Charizard, I don't know if I'm allowed to bring my Meganium"
- "I once again don't think I can bring Meganium into Sneezler"
- "Against Sun, you can't bring Meganium. That's just the rule."
- "Meganium can't touch their Delphox"

But also the reverse — recognizing when he SHOULD have brought it:
- "Meganium is good into five of these Pokemon and just because they have Charizard, I didn't bring it. That doesn't feel correct."

**Key observation:** The star Pokemon is NOT always brought. Having a backup plan (Kangaskhan) is essential. The decision of when to bench the star is a skill that develops through live play.

## PATTERN 9: Endgame Positioning and Win Condition Tracking

Wolfey constantly narrates his endgame win condition:

- "Kangaskhan can always sucker punch Basculegion, but it's not in range right away. We need about 20% more damage."
- "If we hit the rock slide into the Del Fox, then we will win."
- "I have a guaranteed win condition here because I have Scarf Typhlosion in the back and my Aerodactyl still has Focus Sash intact."
- "It's going to come down to whether or not Tauros can solo here."
- Tracking recoil damage as a resource: "The Wave Crash recoil ends up putting it in range of Sucker Punch."

**Key observation:** He's always calculating backwards from "what sequence of events wins me the game" rather than just making the best move this turn.

## PATTERN 11: Acknowledging the Team's Weaknesses Honestly

Wolfey is candid about what his team can't do:

- "I don't really have answers for [Trick Room]"
- "I wish I had Steel Beam" (after struggling vs Floette)
- "I wish I had Tauros" (after a game where he didn't bring it)
- "I think I need Archaludon" (after losing without it)
- "This is a difficult matchup, but I think we ended up getting through it"
- On the Mega Crawdaunt team he tried earlier: "It didn't work. It didn't get to a point where I felt comfortable with it."

**Key observation:** He doesn't pretend the team is perfect. He names weaknesses explicitly and either adapts (swap Aegislash → Archaludon) or accepts them (Trick Room is just hard).

## PATTERN 12: Opponent Prediction and Read-Based Play

Wolfey makes explicit predictions about opponent behavior:

- "I expect them to go for Tailwind plus Flip Turn" — correct
- "They're going to switch, right?" — tracks when opponents should switch
- "I think they should protect Floette. It'd be really risky not to." — correct
- "I think they're going to lock into Last Respects" — reads Choice lock
- "They're going to fake out Tauros" — bets on it and plays accordingly
- "I'm going to call the Rotom to protect here" — explicit read

**Key observation:** He narrates his reads out loud, which means he's making EXPLICIT predictions rather than just reacting. When wrong, he adjusts: "Okay, they outplayed me. That's not good. But it's also not that bad."

## PATTERN 13: Priority and Speed Control as the Central Axis

Speed control dominates his decision-making:

- "I need to get Tailwind up to win this game"
- "Getting rid of their Aerodactyl means Tailwind is expired for them forever"
- "I have to lead Meganium because of the trick room threat"
- "I'm going to tailwind and protect. I hate this play, but I will do it."
- "I needed to go for Tailwind on that turn" (post-loss analysis)
- Aqua Jet (priority) is repeatedly used to finish off threats or break Focus Sash
- Sucker Punch range calculations are constant

**Key observation:** Almost every game-deciding moment revolves around who controls speed. Tailwind timing is the single most important tactical decision.

## PATTERN 14: Archaludon as the Unexpected MVP

Archaludon was a late addition (swapped in for Aegislash between games) and repeatedly overperforms:

- Leftovers + Sturdy creates a healing loop he didn't expect
- Stalwart ignores Follow Me (critical vs Floette teams)
- Dragon Pulse provides safe neutral damage
- Flash Cannon / Steel Beam for Floette specifically
- Thunderbolt for coverage (he says "I really like Thunderbolt Archaludon")
- Survives hits, heals back, activates Sturdy again

**Key observation:** The Pokemon he was most skeptical about ("Yeah, leftovers, whatever") became the most consistently valuable. Empirical testing > theoretical evaluation.

## PATTERN 15: Failed Team Attempts Are Normal

- "I was trying to build a mega Crawdaunt team but it didn't get to a point where I felt comfortable with it"
- He doesn't dwell on the failed attempt — just uses the team he's most confident in
- "The Meganium team out of all the teams I've used is the best one"

**Key observation:** Not every teambuilding attempt works. The skill is knowing when to abandon a concept and go with what's proven.
