# Deckbuilding Strategy Reference

## 1. Deckbuilding Fundamentals

### The 60-Card Framework

| Zone | Count | Role |
|------|-------|------|
| Pokemon | 12-20 | Attackers, draw engines, support abilities |
| Trainers | 26-34 | Draw supporters, search items, switching tools, stadiums |
| Energy | 7-12 | Fuel attacks; special energy fills niche roles |

**Pokemon Role Breakdown**

| Role | Typical Count | Notes |
|------|---------------|-------|
| Main attacker (Stage 1 line) | 2-2 or 3-2 | Basics : evolutions. Mega Pokemon are Stage 1 |
| Main attacker (Stage 2 line) | 4-3-2 or 3-2-2 | Basic-Stage 1-Stage 2. Heavier basic count for consistency |
| Draw engine Pokemon | 2-4 copies | Drakloak (Recon), Lunatone/Solrock, etc. |
| Support/tech Pokemon | 1-2 copies each | Damage boosters, damage movers, bench protection |
| Going-second tech | 0-1 | Bidoof (Itchy Powder item lock) |

**Trainer Breakdown (2026 Post-Rotation Staples)**

| Card | Count | Role |
|------|-------|------|
| Lillie's Termination | 3-4 | Primary draw supporter; refresh hand up to 6 |
| Boss's Orders | 1-3 | Gust an opponent's benched Pokemon to active |
| Crispin | 1-2 | Search deck for 2 basic energy to hand |
| Buddy-Buddy | 4 | Basic Pokemon search (replaced Nest Ball post-rotation) |
| Ultra Ball | 3-4 | Any Pokemon search; discard 2 cards as cost |
| Poke Pad | 3-4 | Recover supporter cards from discard |
| Team Rocket's Handiwork | 0-4 | Search deck for any Trainer card |
| Nice Stretcher | 1-2 | Recover Pokemon from discard |
| Switch / Air Balloon | 1-2 total | Mobility; Air Balloon reduces retreat cost |
| Counter Gain | 0-1 | Reduce attack cost when behind on prizes |
| Stadium cards | 0-2 | Team Rocket's Watch Tower, Jamming Tower, Risky Ruins |

**Energy Count by Strategy**

| Strategy | Energy Count | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Deck with acceleration (Rose's Encouragement, Crispin) | 7-8 | Acceleration recovers or searches energy |
| Standard single-type deck | 7-9 | Mono Fighting, mono Fire, etc. |
| Multi-type deck | 8-12 | Split across types; special energy fills gaps |
| Special energy slots | 1-2 | Legacy Energy, Ignition Energy, Counter Gain |

---

### Consistency Principles

- **4 copies** -- cards you need every game (Lillie's Termination, Buddy-Buddy, Ultra Ball, key attacker's basic)
- **2-3 copies** -- cards you need once per game (Boss's Orders, Crispin, evolution stages)
- **1 copy** -- tech cards, ace spec, counter-specific threats
- Every card must pass two questions:
  - What does this do on **Turn 1**?
  - What does this do in the **late game**?
- Maximize search and draw; redundancy beats luck
- Post-rotation, Poke Pad (supporter recovery) and Buddy-Buddy (basic search) are near-universal 4-ofs

---

### Energy Acceleration

Decks with built-in acceleration run fewer energy than average:

| Acceleration Card | Mechanic | Typical Use |
|-------------------|----------|-------------|
| Rose's Encouragement | Attach 2 basic energy from discard to a Stage 2 Pokemon (only when behind on prizes) | Dragapult ex fueling Phantom Dive from discard |
| Crispin | Search deck for 2 basic energy to hand | Universal; powers up multi-type attackers in one supporter use |
| Ethan's Adventure | Search deck for any combination of Ethan's Pokemon and Fire energy | Ethan's Typhlosion line; doubles as discard-pile fuel |
| Counter Gain | Reduce attack cost by 1 Colorless when behind on prizes | Cheap attackers like Diggersby attack for free |
| Energy Switch | Move a basic energy between your Pokemon | Redirect energy to Mankey Dory or backup attacker |

## 2. Deckbuilding Principles

*Principles extracted from 4 City League tournament-winning decks (2026, Perfect Order format): Dragapult ex, Mega Starmie ex, Ethan's Typhlosion, and Diggersby.*

### Every Deck Needs a Core Mechanism

A winning deck is built around a central engine -- one interaction that defines every game. Everything else in the 60 cards exists to enable, protect, or amplify that engine.

| Deck | Core Mechanism | How It Works |
|------|----------------|--------------|
| Dragapult ex | Phantom Dive spread | 200 to active + place 6 damage counters anywhere on bench. Sets up multi-KO turns by softening bench targets over multiple attacks |
| Mega Starmie ex | Jetting Blow snipe + chip | 120 to active + 50 to a bench target. Layered with Freezing Shroud, Risky Ruins, and Oranguru damage movement to accumulate lethal math across the board |
| Ethan's Typhlosion | Discard scaling | Body Blast deals 40 + 60 per Ethan's Adventure in discard (max 280). The entire deck is structured to dump supporters into discard as fast as possible |
| Diggersby | Self-damage conversion | Earthquake (140) hits your own bench for 30 each. Mankey Dory converts that self-damage into opponent damage, turning a drawback into 200-230+ effective output |

**The principle:** Before adding any card, ask: "Does this serve the core mechanism?" If a card does not set up, fuel, or amplify your central engine, it probably does not belong. A focused 60 cards always beats a scattered one.

---

### Building Your Draw Engine

Every winning deck runs a draw engine beyond just supporter cards. Supporters are once-per-turn; Pokemon-based draw lets you see extra cards every turn without using your supporter.

| Draw Engine | Type | How It Works | Used In |
|-------------|------|--------------|---------|
| Drakloak (Recon) | Pokemon ability | Look at top 2 cards, keep 1, bottom 1. Stacks with multiple Drakloak | Dragapult ex, Ethan's Typhlosion |
| Lunatone / Solrock (Luna Cycle) | Pokemon ability pair | Discard a Fighting energy to draw cards. Self-sustaining with Fighting energy base | Diggersby |
| Meowth EX (Last Resort Catch) | Pokemon ability (on bench entry) | Search deck for a supporter card when played from hand to bench | Dragapult ex, Ethan's Typhlosion, Mega Starmie ex |
| Noctowl | Pokemon ability | Search deck for 2 Trainer cards when a Terror Pokemon is in play | Dragapult ex |

**The principle:** Your draw supporter (Lillie's Termination) is your floor. Your Pokemon-based draw engine is your ceiling. Decks that rely only on supporters for draw will stall whenever they miss a supporter or need their supporter use for something else (Crispin, Boss's Orders). Build a draw engine that operates independently.

**Why Drakloak appears in multiple decks:** Drakloak's Recon ability is so strong that even Ethan's Typhlosion (a Fire deck) splashes the Dreepy/Drakloak line purely for draw power. The Dragapult evolution is a bonus attacker, not the reason for including the line in Typhlosion builds.

---

### Support Pokemon Roles

Beyond your attacker and draw engine, winning decks include support Pokemon that fill specific roles. Not every deck needs every role, but knowing the menu helps you build.

**Damage Boosters** -- increase your attack output

| Card | Effect | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| Victini (Victory Cheer) | Fire evolution attacks do +10 damage | Typhlosion's Body Blast 280 becomes 290 |
| Break Bangle | Tool: +30 damage; -30 HP to Stage 2 Pokemon | Typhlosion at 290 + 30 = 320, one-shotting most Stage 2 EX |
| Dusknoir | Place 13 damage counters on opponent's Pokemon | Dragapult's 200 + 130 Dusknoir = 330, KO range for most format |

**Damage Movers** -- relocate damage counters from your Pokemon to opponent's

| Card | Effect | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| Mankey Dory | Move damage counters from your Pokemon to opponent's (requires Dark energy) | Appears in Dragapult, Diggersby, and Mega Starmie. Turns self-damage and chip into real damage on opponent |
| Oranguru | Attach Dark energy, move damage from your Pokemon to opponent's | Mega Starmie: turns Jetting Blow's 120 into 150+ by moving accumulated damage |

**Passive Damage / Spread** -- add damage without attacking

| Card | Effect | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| Froslass (Freezing Shroud) | During Pokemon Checkup, place 1 damage counter on each Pokemon with an ability | Mega Starmie: chips down Meowth EX and other ability-dependent bench sitters |
| Risky Ruins | Stadium: place 20 damage on any non-Dark Pokemon when benched | Mega Starmie: 20 (Risky Ruins) + 50 (Jetting Blow snipe) = 70, KOs many basics |

**Bench Protection**

| Card | Effect | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| Shaymin | Prevent damage to your benched Pokemon | Typhlosion: protects Drakloak and Quilava from bench snipe |

**The principle:** Your support Pokemon should form a synergy loop with your core mechanism. Diggersby deliberately damages its own bench, so Mankey Dory turns that into upside. Mega Starmie spreads chip damage everywhere, so Froslass and Risky Ruins add even more chip. Match your support to your engine.

### The Prize Trade

The prize trade is the most important strategic consideration in deckbuilding. Every KO your opponent takes gives them prize cards. The player who takes 6 prizes first wins. Your deck's prize math determines how many KOs each player needs.

**Multi-prize attackers (EX / Mega Pokemon)**

| Attacker | Prizes Given Up | KOs Opponent Needs |
|----------|----------------|--------------------|
| Mega Starmie ex (330 HP) | 3 | 2 Mega KOs = 6 prizes = game over |
| Dragapult ex (320 HP) | 2 | 3 EX KOs = 6 prizes |
| Meowth EX (170 HP) | 2 | Easy snipe target on bench |

**Single-prize attackers (non-EX)**

| Attacker | Prizes Given Up | KOs Opponent Needs |
|----------|----------------|--------------------|
| Ethan's Typhlosion (170 HP) | 1 | 6 KOs to win |
| Diggersby (150 HP) | 1 | 6 KOs to win |

**How Legacy Energy changes the math:** Mega Starmie runs Legacy Energy (ace spec) to reduce prizes given up by 1 when the attached Mega is KO'd. This means one Mega KO gives 2 prizes instead of 3, forcing the opponent to find a third KO somewhere. This single card can extend the game by an entire turn.

**The principle:** Single-prize decks (Typhlosion, Diggersby) force opponents to take 6 separate KOs. This gives you more turns to execute your game plan and more opportunities to use comeback tools like Unfair Stamp or Counter Gain. Multi-prize decks must compensate with higher HP, faster setup, or tools like Legacy Energy that manipulate the exchange. When building, always count: "How many KOs does my opponent need to win, and how many do I need?"

### Tech Slots and Going-Second Tools

Tech cards are 1-of inclusions that address specific matchups, provide a going-second advantage, or offer a surprise option. They do not appear in every game, but when they matter, they are decisive.

**Going-Second Tools**

| Card | Effect | Used In |
|------|--------|---------|
| Bidoof (Itchy Powder) | Prevents opponent from playing Item cards on their next turn | Dragapult, Mega Starmie, Ethan's Typhlosion |
| Precious Treasure | Ace spec: fill your bench with basic Pokemon from deck | Diggersby (explosive turn-1 bench setup) |

Bidoof appears in 3 of the 4 tournament-winning decks. Going second is often considered a disadvantage because the first player gets to evolve sooner. Bidoof flips this by shutting down the first player's item cards (Ultra Ball, Buddy-Buddy, Poke Pad) on their critical second turn. Some decks (like Dragapult) actively prefer going second for this reason.

**Disruption Supporters and Stadiums**

| Card | Effect | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| Team Rocket's Watch Tower | Stadium: prevents bench abilities from triggering searches | Blocks Kangaskhan, Noctowl, Meowth EX. Run in Dragapult, Typhlosion |
| Jamming Tower | Stadium: all Pokemon tools have no effect | Dragapult runs this alongside no tools of its own |
| Judge | Shuffle both hands, draw 4 each | Hand disruption; tech against Alakazam decks |

**The principle:** Tech slots should answer the question: "What beats my deck, and what 1-card answer exists?" If Battle Cage shuts down your bench damage, consider running a stadium to bump it. If Alakazam is popular, include Judge. Tech slots are meta-dependent and should be the first cards you swap between tournaments.

---

### Ace Spec Selection

You may include exactly one Ace Spec card in your deck. The right choice depends on your deck's strategy, not on the card's power in isolation.

| Ace Spec | Strategy Fit | Used In |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Unfair Stamp | Comeback decks that expect to trade KOs. When your Pokemon is KO'd, opponent goes to 2 cards, you draw 5. Best in decks that plan to lose attackers (Typhlosion, Dragapult) | Dragapult ex, Ethan's Typhlosion |
| Precious Treasure | Fill bench from deck. Best in decks that need many bench Pokemon set up fast (multiple Mankey Dory, Lunatone/Solrock engine) | Diggersby |
| Legacy Energy | Reduces prizes given up by 1. Best in Mega/EX-heavy decks where 2 KOs would end the game. Also provides any-type energy | Mega Starmie ex |

**The principle:** Your ace spec should solve your deck's biggest structural problem. Diggersby needs 4+ bench Pokemon operational by turn 2, so Precious Treasure is essential. Typhlosion expects to lose attackers, so Unfair Stamp turns each loss into hand disruption. Mega Starmie gives up 3 prizes per KO, so Legacy Energy prevents a 2-KO loss.

### Energy Strategy

**Mono-type vs. Multi-type**

| Approach | Pros | Cons | Example |
|----------|------|------|---------|
| Mono-type + splash | Consistent energy hits; can run type-specific search (Fighting Gong) | Limited attacker options | Diggersby: 7 Fighting + 4 Dark (Dark is splash for Mankey Dory) |
| Dual-type | Access to two attacker types | Harder to find the right energy each turn; needs Crispin | Dragapult: 3 Psychic + 3 Fire + 2 Dark |
| Multi-type with special energy | Maximum flexibility | More complex; special energy can be removed | Mega Starmie: 4 Dark + 3 Water + 1 Ignition + 1 Legacy |

**Typical energy counts from winning decks:**

| Deck | Total Energy | Breakdown |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| Diggersby | 11 | 7 Fighting, 4 Dark |
| Dragapult ex | 8 | 3 Psychic, 3 Fire, 2 Dark |
| Ethan's Typhlosion | 7 | 5 Fire, 2 Psychic |
| Mega Starmie ex | 9 | 4 Dark, 3 Water, 1 Ignition, 1 Legacy |

**Energy acceleration is often more important than energy count.** Dragapult runs only 8 energy but has Rose's Encouragement (attach 2 from discard to a Stage 2) and Crispin (search 2 to hand). Typhlosion runs only 7 but Ethan's Adventure searches both Pokemon and energy. The fewer energy you run, the more acceleration and search you need.

**The Counter Gain trick:** When your deck uses cheap attacks (Diggersby's Earthquake costs 1 Colorless), Counter Gain makes attacks free when behind on prizes. This frees your energy attachment for the turn to go on support Pokemon like Mankey Dory instead. This is especially powerful in single-prize decks that will frequently be behind on prizes.

**The principle:** Match your energy count to your acceleration. Run type-specific search (Fighting Gong, Energy Searcher) to find splash colors. Always include Dark energy if running Mankey Dory / Oranguru damage movers.

## 4. Advanced Deckbuilding Theory

### Two-Layer Draw Architecture

Every competitive deck needs draw support on two independent layers:

**Layer 1 — Supporters (the floor):** Lillie's Termination, Professor's Research, Iono. Run 8-10 total draw Supporters for ~80-88% probability of having one in your opening 8 cards. This is the minimum each turn.

**Layer 2 — Pokemon abilities (the ceiling):** Drakloak (Recon), Lunatone/Solrock (Luna Cycle), Gholdengo (Coin Bonus), Dudunsparce (Run Away Draw), Kirlia (Refinement). These provide repeatable draw that doesn't consume the Supporter slot.

**The ceiling determines how much a deck can accomplish beyond the single-Supporter minimum.** Decks with strong Pokemon-based draw can use their Supporter slot for Boss's Orders or Crispin while maintaining card flow. Decks without it stall whenever they need a non-draw Supporter.

**Search redundancy across both layers should total 8-12 cards** that can find other cards (Ultra Ball, Buddy-Buddy, Pokegear, type-specific search). This prevents "dead draws" — hands with no actionable cards.

Use Limitless TCG's DrawCalc (limitlesstcg.com/tools/drawcalc) or pokemoncard.io's sample hand simulator to test whether your draw architecture achieves acceptable consistency thresholds.

### The Supporter Tempo Bottleneck

You can only play one Supporter per turn. This is the core resource tension in competitive TCG: every Supporter use is an opportunity cost against every other Supporter.

| You need to... | Supporter cost |
|----------------|---------------|
| Draw cards | Lillie's Termination |
| Search energy | Crispin |
| Gust a target | Boss's Orders |
| Disrupt opponent | Judge |

Pokemon abilities that replicate Supporter effects (Meowth EX searching a Supporter, Drakloak providing draw) break this bottleneck by letting you accomplish two tasks in one turn.

### Deck Thinning and Sequencing

**Deck thinning is real card advantage.** Cards like Trekking Shoes and Gutsy Pickaxe that cycle themselves effectively reduce your deck size, improving the probability of drawing impactful cards on future turns. Ultra Ball also thins (discard 2 to search 1).

**Play order within a turn matters.** Use search effects (Ultra Ball) before draw effects (Supporter) to thin the deck first. Use abilities that look at the top of your deck (Drakloak's Recon) after shuffling effects, not before. Bench Pokemon with come-into-play abilities (Meowth EX) early to maximize triggers.

### Probability Thinking ("Outs")

An "out" is any card remaining in your deck that advances your game state. Running 4 copies of a card gives roughly 40% chance of seeing it in your opening 7-card hand. Running 8 combined search/draw cards that find it pushes effective probability much higher.

**Mulligan probability is a deckbuilding constraint.** Running fewer Basic Pokemon increases the chance of revealing cards through forced mulligans. Top decks run 8-14 Basic Pokemon to keep mulligan probability under 5%.

## 5. Archetype Classification

### Strategic Patterns

While Pokemon TCG archetypes are named by their primary Pokemon (Gardevoir ex, Dragapult ex), the underlying strategic patterns recur across formats:

| Pattern | Description | Coaching Advice |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Direct Aggression** | Take KOs as fast as possible; stream attackers | "Every turn without an attack is a turn you fall behind." |
| **Spread** | Accumulate damage across multiple targets, then take multi-KOs in burst turns | "Patience is your weapon. Let damage accumulate, then close 2-3 prizes in one turn." |
| **Control** | Cut off opponent's avenues to victory: energy denial, item lock, hand disruption | "Your goal isn't to attack fast; it's to make your opponent unable to attack." |
| **Mill** | Win by decking out the opponent | "Count your opponent's deck constantly." |
| **Engine Combo** | Rely on specific card combinations to generate overwhelming advantage | Setup-dependent but unstoppable once assembled. |

### Recurring Engine Patterns

| Pattern | Examples |
|---------|---------|
| Ability-based draw | Drakloak, Lunatone/Solrock, Gholdengo, Dudunsparce |
| Energy acceleration | Gardevoir (discard), Charizard (Infernal Reign), Grimmsnarl (Punk Up) |
| Search engines | Pidgeot ex (Quick Search), Miraidon (Tandem Unit) |
| Discard-fueled scaling | Raging Bolt (energy), Ethan's Typhlosion (supporters), Gholdengo (energy from hand) |
| Passive chip damage | Froslass (Freezing Shroud), Risky Ruins stadium |
| Damage movement | Munkidori, Mankey Dory, Oranguru, Dusknoir |

## 6. Counter-Meta Theory

### The Self-Correcting Metagame

When one deck dominates, counter-strategies emerge. Being the most-played deck makes you the most-targeted deck. This is why Dragapult Dusknoir (the most-played deck in Standard) has declining win rates — the meta has adapted to beat it.

### Reading the Metagame

Use Limitless TCG (limitlesstcg.com/decks) as the primary metagame data source:

1. **Filter by tournament tier and recency.** Regional/International/Worlds data reflects the most competitive metagame. League Challenge data is noisier.
2. **Compare meta share vs win rate.** High share + low WR = "popular but solved." Low share + high WR = underplayed/breakout.
3. **Read matchup tables.** A deck's overall win rate matters less than its spread against the top 5-8 meta decks.
4. **Watch for the "Week 1" effect.** After a new set drops, the meta features experimentation and unoptimized lists. It settles after 2-3 major events.

### No Sideboard — A Defining Feature

Unlike other TCGs, Pokemon TCG has no sideboard. The 60 cards registered at the start of a tournament are the only cards for every match. This means:
- Every tech slot competes with consistency cards
- Metagame prediction is more consequential
- Tech choices must be maindecked, creating a sharper consistency-vs-coverage tradeoff

### Evaluating Deck Viability

A deck is structurally good (not just winning by surprise) if it:
1. Maintains positive win rate (>50%) after 500+ recorded matches
2. Has favorable matchups against at least 2 of the top 5 most-played decks
3. Doesn't have any extreme unfavorable matchup (>65%) against a high-share deck
4. Has been piloted to day 2 by multiple different players
5. Survives a meta shift where opponents know to expect it

## 7. Testing Methodology

### Testing Stages

| Stage | What to Do | Purpose |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| 1. Goldfishing | Play alone; no opponent | Identify consistency issues — "Your deck will never win if it can't function" |
| 2. Digital testing | PTCGL or simulators | Rapid iteration without physical card changes |
| 3. Tracked playtesting | Play against others; track 4 questions per game | Identify systemic patterns |
| 4. Focused matchup testing | Test against top 8 meta decks from Limitless | Validate matchup spread |

### The Four Questions (After Every Game)

1. Which needed cards didn't appear?
2. Which cards appeared too frequently?
3. Did my attackers win the prize trade?
4. What cards did I wish were included?

### The 10-Game Rule

"If you play ten games and you still haven't used a card — you should probably drop it." Adjust counts before cutting concepts (4→3, or 2→3). Only change the strategy when the core mechanism is fundamentally flawed against the expected meta.

### Common Coaching Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | What Happens | Fix |
|-------------|-------------|-----|
| No core mechanism | Deck is a collection of good cards with no central engine | Ask: "What is this deck's one interaction that defines every game?" |
| Too few draw supporters | 2-3 copies instead of 8-10 total draw/search supporters | Run 4 Lillie's Termination + Pokemon-based draw engine |
| No Pokemon-based draw | Relying only on Supporters stalls whenever you need Boss/Crispin | Add Drakloak line, Lunatone/Solrock, or equivalent |
| Ignoring the prize trade | Not counting KOs needed to win | Always count: "How many KOs does each side need?" |
| Over-teching | 10 different 1-of techs, only 6 Supporters | Every tech replaces a consistency card. Prioritize consistency. |
| Under-testing / results-oriented | Changing deck after one bad loss | Use the 10-game rule. One game is not a sample. |
| Multi-type energy scatter | 3-4 energy types hoping for flexibility | Start mono-type + one splash color maximum |
| Cool cards over consistent cards | Flashy 1-ofs that rarely appear | 4-of cards you need every game; 1-of only for targeted tech |
